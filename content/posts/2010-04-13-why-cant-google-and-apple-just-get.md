Google and Apple are at war.&nbsp; Google entered Apple's 'home turf' with <a href="http://www.android.com/">Android</a>, and Apple is entering Google's 'home turf' with <a href="http://www.apple.com/iphone/preview-iphone-os/">iAd</a>.&nbsp; There is no question that the war is on. <br />
<br />
In most situations, competition is a great thing.&nbsp; It drives companies to innovate and produce better products and services.&nbsp; While that will certainly happen with their respective mobile operating systems in this situation, I believe these are two companies that would be much stronger working together.<br />
<br />
Google and Apple are good at very different things.&nbsp; Gruber nails it when <a href="http://daringfireball.net/linked/2010/04/12/google-docs">he says</a>:<br />
<blockquote>No better comparison of the cultural differences between Google and  Apple than to compare Google Docs and iWork. iWork has no form of cloud  based syncing or collaboration; the appeal of the apps (both on the Mac  and iPad) is that it helps you create beautiful documents. Google Docs  is all about cloud-based syncing and collaboration; its example  documents are downright homely.</blockquote>Google is great at building services that scale.&nbsp; Gmail, Google Docs, and Google Maps are all great services, usually with simple and effective interfaces.&nbsp; Apple is great at creating intuitive user interfaces and well engineered hardware.&nbsp; As an end user, I want my services provided by Google on hardware built by Apple.<br />
<br />
More specifically, I want my web user interfaces built by Google, and my native applications built by Apple.&nbsp; I enjoy the close integration I have on my iPhone between the Calendar, Mail, and Contact applications and Google Apps (which is ironically made possible by Microsoft's ActiveSync).&nbsp; I like Google's web interface to Gmail and Calendar.&nbsp; I want them both, and I want them to work together.<br />
<br />
In my ideal world, Google would provide a service interface for these services, and a generic web interface that is accessible anywhere, on any (modern) browser.&nbsp; Apple (and others) would provide native applications that utilize these services.&nbsp; I want to be able to access my data using either the browser (provided by Google) or a native app (by Apple) on either my iPhone or MacBook.&nbsp; I wish the Address Book application on my MacBook worked as well with Gmail as the Contacts app does on my iPhone.<br />
<br />
It should work this way across all services.&nbsp; iWork should be able to store and edit files on Google Docs, which could then be edited using the web interface as well.&nbsp; Everything lives in the cloud, with the ability to cache local copies with the native applications (or advanced browsers). &nbsp; <br />
<br />
Unfortunately, it looks like Apple and Google are moving farther apart, instead of closer together.&nbsp; It is clear that Google first 'invaded' Apple's space with Android, but Apple is certainly a company that holds grudges and is very aggressive. &nbsp; Adobe knows this all too well.<br />
<br />
As an end user, I'm afraid this is one area where competition may not produce the best result, but who knows.&nbsp; Neither Google nor Apple are going away anytime soon, and they may both surprise us with what they do.&nbsp; Let's just hope it is about creating better products instead of damaging the competition.

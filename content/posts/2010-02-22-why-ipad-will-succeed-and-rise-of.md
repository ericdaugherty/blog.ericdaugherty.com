The iPad is an appliance, and it will be successful.&nbsp; But before we get to that, we need to start at the beginning.<br />
<br />
When I was a kid I ran a dial-up BBS service (The Outhouse) on a computer cobbled together from old donated computers and a few new parts I'd purchased.&nbsp; I would take apart old computers (donated by my friends parents after their companies discarded them) and test the various parts for something I could scavenge.&nbsp; I spent hours pouring over the massive Computer Shopper magazine to find the best deal on a new hard drive or modem.&nbsp; This was the very definition of an (economy) do-it-yourself general purpose computer.<br />
<br />
In my first two decades of computing I never purchased a pre-built computer.&nbsp; I always assembled new computers from parts, or upgrade my existing computer (often replacing everything but the case and CD-ROM drive).&nbsp; <a href="http://pricewatch.com/">Pricewatch.com</a> was my favorite site for a long time.<br />
<br />
But along came a new device that started a big change, <a href="http://www.tivo.com/">TiVo</a>.&nbsp; I bought my first TiVo around the year 2000.&nbsp; It was something I could have built myself, but I realized that the convenience of having a dedicated appliance was worth the cost.&nbsp; I just wanted it to work, and it did. Very well.<br />
<br />
In the years since, I've been transitioning away from general purpose computers to appliances.&nbsp; The Linux and Windows desktop/servers I used to run (24/7) have been replaced by a Linux based wireless router (<a href="http://rcm.amazon.com/e/cm?lt1=_blank&amp;bc1=000000&amp;IS2=1&amp;bg1=FFFFFF&amp;fc1=000000&amp;lc1=0000FF&amp;t=ericdaugcom-20&amp;o=1&amp;p=8&amp;l=as1&amp;m=amazon&amp;f=ifr&amp;md=10FE9736YVPPT7A0FBG2&amp;asins=B000BTL0OA">Linksys WRT 54 GL</a>) and a NAS (<a href="http://rcm.amazon.com/e/cm?lt1=_blank&amp;bc1=000000&amp;IS2=1&amp;bg1=FFFFFF&amp;fc1=000000&amp;lc1=0000FF&amp;t=ericdaugcom-20&amp;o=1&amp;p=8&amp;l=as1&amp;m=amazon&amp;f=ifr&amp;md=10FE9736YVPPT7A0FBG2&amp;asins=B000VA3TXY">ReadyNAS NV+</a>).&nbsp; They provide nearly all the services the old general purpose computers provided with a few exceptions, and each of those exceptions have been moved to the cloud.&nbsp; I've moved my website hosting and email hosting to the cloud using GoDaddy (&lt; $5 month for web hosting) and Google Apps (Free).&nbsp; They provide a better quality of service, at a minimal cost.&nbsp; Along the way I migrated from writing and hosting my own email server (<a href="http://www.ericdaugherty.com/java/mailserver/">Java Email Server</a>), to hosting email at GoDaddy, to free hosting at Google.&nbsp; That is quite a shift in effort and cost.<br />
<br />
The same progression is true with my other devices.&nbsp; I now exclusively use laptops, and have not assembled a desktop more than 4 years.&nbsp; I've also embraced Apple devices, which have more of an appliance feel than do other devices.&nbsp; I use iTunes to manage my music, a couple of <a href="http://rcm.amazon.com/e/cm?lt1=_blank&amp;bc1=000000&amp;IS2=1&amp;bg1=FFFFFF&amp;fc1=000000&amp;lc1=0000FF&amp;t=ericdaugcom-20&amp;o=1&amp;p=8&amp;l=as1&amp;m=amazon&amp;f=ifr&amp;md=10FE9736YVPPT7A0FBG2&amp;asins=B0015YJOK2">iTunes Airport Express</a> units to listen to music on my stereos, and an iPhone as my mobile music device, and phone.&nbsp; While one could argue that these are not really a change to appliances, I think they match the general trend.&nbsp; Appliances provide a pre-defined (somewhat inflexible) experience that 'just works' as long as you stay in the provided feature set.&nbsp; This is exactly what Apple excels at doing.<br />
<br />
Finally, I've adopted a <a href="http://rcm.amazon.com/e/cm?lt1=_blank&amp;bc1=000000&amp;IS2=1&amp;bg1=FFFFFF&amp;fc1=000000&amp;lc1=0000FF&amp;t=ericdaugcom-20&amp;o=1&amp;p=8&amp;l=as1&amp;m=amazon&amp;f=ifr&amp;md=10FE9736YVPPT7A0FBG2&amp;asins=B0015T963C">Kindle</a>.&nbsp; While I could read on a laptop, or an iPhone, this single purpose device excels at linear reading (ie Books).&nbsp; I use it every day.<br />
<br />
There are several reasons for this trend.&nbsp; One is simple economics.&nbsp; As the years go buy, I have more discretionary income, and more demands on my time (namely two young children).&nbsp; The convenience of buying appliances versus tinkering has certainly changed for me.<br />
<br />
I think there is more to the story though.&nbsp; As the computing and home electronic fields mature, it becomes easier and more cost effective to create appliances that fit into our worlds.&nbsp; Which brings us to the iPad.<br />
<br />
The iPad is not the first device to attempt to move the general purpose computing environment to an appliance.&nbsp; One could argue that it is really a descendant of the <a href="http://www.msntv.com/pc/">WebTV</a> concept.&nbsp; Take the primary activities people use general purpose computers for and put them into an appliance.&nbsp; This brings up a brief and interesting digression... <br />
<br />
Apple does not create markets.&nbsp; Apple waits until a market is ready, and then delivers a product with impressive polish and ease of use.&nbsp; MacBooks don't do anything a similarly priced PC can't do, they are just prettier and easier to use (or have been traditionally).&nbsp; The iPod was not the first portable MP3 player, it was just better (including the iTunes ecosystem).&nbsp; The iPhone didn't break new ground on smart phone functionality, it was just better (again, including the iTunes and AppStore ecosystem).&nbsp; Finally, the iPad isn't new either.&nbsp; Microsoft has had a TabletPC version of their Operating System since 2001.&nbsp; The previously mentioned <a href="http://www.msntv.com/pc/">WebTV</a> provided email and web browsing as an appliance experience.<br />
<br />
Apple is attempting to build on these ideas, with Apple's traditional polish, iTunes ecosystem, and of course, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality_distortion_field">Reality Distortion Field</a>.&nbsp; I don't know that version 1 of the iPad will be a success, but I am convinced that appliance computing will become a significant mainstream success.<br />
<br />
Final Note to Developers: As Software Developers, we will always use a general purpose computer.&nbsp; Just as a carpenter utilizes a set of tools to build a house, we will utilize a set of tools (ie general purpose computers) to build appliances.&nbsp; Our goal should always be building applications in the appliance mindset.&nbsp; My youngest child (2 years old) can turn on my iPhone and open her favorite puzzle game.&nbsp; All of our computing experience should be this easy.

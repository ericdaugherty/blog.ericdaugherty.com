There is no arguing that the market share of devices built on the Open Source Android operating system is impressive.&nbsp; The Android platform, judged by adoption, is a success.<br />
<br />
But who are the winners?<br />
<br />
Google, who is spending the money to develop and market Android, obviously hopes to gain from their effort.&nbsp; While they do not sell Android, they support it to foster more traffic to Google and the web in general, which will, in theory, sell more ads.&nbsp; When asked about the ad revenue from Android, CEO Eric Schmidt said: <a href="http://www.ibtimes.com/articles/42246/20100810/google-apple-revenue-business-model-android-open-source.htm">"Trust me that revenue is large enough to pay for all of the Android activities and a whole bunch more."</a>&nbsp; Google’s Jonathan Rosenberg estimated <a href="http://www.eurodroid.com/2010/10/google-android-revenue-now-running-at-1bn-per-year/">Google's Android related revenue at $1b annually</a>.&nbsp; However, this revenue is all indirect.&nbsp; Would Google be just as well off if the smart phone market were entirely iPhone devices defaulted to use Google and YouTube?<br />
<br />
But Android is beginning to be used in ways that would appear to be neutral or negative to Google.&nbsp; Rumors swirled in early September that <a href="http://blogs.computerworld.com/16917/with_the_verizon_bing_deal_has_microsoft_found_a_chink_in_googles_android_armor">Verizon was replacing Google Search with Bing</a> on all of its Android phones.&nbsp; Those were followed up quickly with denials, stating that <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/microsoft-bing-android-phones-2010-9">Bing would not be used on ALL Android phones</a>, but that it would be the default on some.&nbsp; So Verizon is shipping some Android phones defaulted to use Bing search.&nbsp; Also, Google competitor <a href="http://www.asymco.com/2010/11/19/did-google-arm-its-own-enemies-with-android/">Baidu is reportedly working to build Android powered smart phones with all Google references replaced with Baidu</a>.&nbsp; Neither of these uses will drive revenue, direct or indirect, to Google.&nbsp; These examples illustrate Android's openness.&nbsp; No one, not even Google, can control how Android is used.<br />
<br />
I believe Android is, and will continue to be, a success for Google.&nbsp; The various other uses of Android do not diminish the value of the normal uses of Android.&nbsp; The existence of Android certainly provides a better situation for Google than a smart phone market dominated by Apple and Microsoft alone.

Microsoft has been struggling to find a new line of revenue that will 'move the needle' for more than a decade. &nbsp;Their stock price has gone down 8% relative to 10 years ago. &nbsp;As a comparison:

- AAPL (Apple): +3,701
- AMZN (Amazon) : +903%
- GM (General Motors): +8%
- PG (Proctor and Gamble): +87%
<div><a href="http://www.google.com/finance?chdnp=1&amp;chdd=1&amp;chds=1&amp;chdv=1&amp;chvs=maximized&amp;chdeh=0&amp;chfdeh=0&amp;chdet=1295650951616&amp;chddm=491096&amp;chls=IntervalBasedLine&amp;cmpto=NASDAQ:AAPL;NASDAQ:AMZN;NYSE:GM;NYSE:PG&amp;cmptdms=0;0;0;0&amp;q=NASDAQ:MSFT&amp;ntsp=0">Source</a> (1/21/2010)</div><div>

</div><div>Not only have&nbsp;technology&nbsp;companies wildly outperformed Microsoft so have large US stalwarts like GM and Proctor and Gamble. &nbsp;GM even pulled that off despite&nbsp;<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Motors_Chapter_11_reorganization">bankruptcy&nbsp;protection</a>! &nbsp;Microsoft is struggling for Growth.</div><div>

</div><div>Microsoft's revenue by division in the latest reported quarter (Q2 2011 - Ending 9/30/2010):</div><div>
- Windows and Windows Live: $4.76B
- Server &amp; Tools: $3.96B
- Business: $5.126B
- Online services: $0.527B
- Entertainment &amp; Devices: $1.8B
</div><div><a href="http://www.betanews.com/joewilcox/article/Microsoft-Q1-2011-by-the-numbers-Beats-consensus-but-not-Apple/1288297586">Source</a></div><div>

</div><div>The vast majority of Microsoft's income comes from the same place it has for years: Windows, Windows Server, and Office/Exchange.</div><div>

</div><div>There is little growth potential for these businesses (at least in the Western world) as they are nearly a monopoly in the Windows and Office/Exchange business lines. &nbsp;And these lines of business are under new attacks on several fronts.</div><div>

</div><div>Windows: Mac OSX, Netbooks, iPads, Android Tablets (soon), and Chrome OS (soon) are or soon will be increasing their market share. &nbsp;While it is not one to one, the growth in this area will diminish the Windows market share.</div><div>

</div><div>Office/Exchange: The advent of hosted solutions,&nbsp;primarily&nbsp;from Google in the form of Google Apps (Email + Office) enables smaller (and now medium size to large) companies to utilize Google as an alternative to the Office/Exchange monopoly.</div><div>

</div><div>Microsoft has focused on new fronts, such as Search (Bing), Mobile (Windows Phone 7), and Gaming (XBox) to provide growth (or backfill slowing growth). &nbsp;But when you look at the current picture, less than 15% of its revenue comes from outside of the three major cash cows.</div><div>

</div><div>The picture looks even worse when you contrast it with Apple. &nbsp;In Apples most recent quarter (one after the above MS quarter), Apple reported $2.79B in revenue from the iPad. &nbsp;The iPad, less than a year old, has already surpassed the revenue of all of Microsoft's new endeavors.

(<a href="http://www.betanews.com/joewilcox/article/Apple-Q4-2010-by-the-numbers-Record-iPhone-sales-and-iPad-push-revenue-to-2034-billion/1287432873">Apple revenue source</a>)

So Microsoft, in a decade of attempting to find new revenue, has come up with less revenue than Apple has in the 3rd quarter of a new product that was <a href="http://daringfireball.net/linked/2011/01/18/claim-chowder-ipad-sales-2010">nearly universally shrugged off by the tech reporters</a>.

Microsoft needs to launch an iPad sized product every other year to regain anything near its previous growth. &nbsp;Apple has done that for nearly the entire decade. &nbsp;Microsoft has maybe done it once, with the XBox 360, after many years of&nbsp;investment.

This is why I think MSFT will still be a $30 stock in a decade, while Apple will have another decade of 4 digit percent growth.</div>

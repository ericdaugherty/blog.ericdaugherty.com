Two interesting acquisitions were announced today, one exciting, one disappointing.<br />
<br />
First, the exciting one.&nbsp; <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/venturecapital/2010/04/28/apple-moves-deeper-into-voice-activated-search-with-siri-buy/?mod=rss_WSJBlog">Apple acquired Siri</a>.&nbsp; I've used the Siri app for the iPhone, and was very impressed.&nbsp; It attempts tries to be your digital secretary, and actually does it quite well.&nbsp; It abstracts you away from how it finds out information, and just presents what you want to know.&nbsp; It works great for the simple cases so far, and I think over time will become very good at much more.&nbsp; For Apple, who I believe is focusing on making computers into appliances, this is a great fit.&nbsp; As I discussed in my post about the <a href="http://blog.ericdaugherty.com/2010/02/why-ipad-will-succeed-and-rise-of.html">iPad</a>, I believe the next evolution of the computing space is creating computing appliances, not computers.&nbsp; Thinking of the iPad as a computer with a touch screen instead of a computer with a keyboard is wrong, just as thinking of a TiVo as a computer with a video capture card is wrong.&nbsp; Yes, both analogies are technically correct, but they both miss the point.&nbsp; Both are computing appliances, not computers, and the rules and expectations for them should be different.&nbsp; I'm very excited to see what Apple will do with Siri.<br />
<br />
And now for the disappointing one.&nbsp; <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/04/29/technology/29palm.html">HP acquires Palm</a>.&nbsp; Palm was once a great company.&nbsp; They nearly single handily created the PDA (Personal Digital Assistant) market, with the Palm Pilot.&nbsp; The Palm Pilot dominated the market for years, until the advent of smart phones.&nbsp; Then they created the smartphone market with their Treo line (technically they bought Handspring, which was formed by the same folks), and extended their domination.&nbsp; I was an early adopter back in 1998 and used Palm Pilots and Treos up until last year when I switched to an iPhone 3GS.<br />
<br />
If was very excited about Palm's new OS, but in the end it was too little, too late.&nbsp; If they had launched the WebOS/Pre a year before the iPhone came out, they may still be dominating the landscape.&nbsp; And they certainly should have been able to do that.&nbsp; The Palm OS was great in the 90's, worked OK in the early '00's, but was really showing its age by 2005.&nbsp; They waited far to long to move to the next generation.<br />
<br />
I don't see how being acquired by HP will change their position.&nbsp; iPhone and Android are locked arm-in-arm for the smartphone market.&nbsp; iPhone owns the proprietary walled garden space, and Android is the open, extensible choice.&nbsp; Microsoft and RIM are still hanging around, mostly in the corporate market.&nbsp; There just isn't room for Palm.<br />
<br />
HP is not an innovative company today, and is more known for their existing relationships and sales channels than engineering.&nbsp; While I don't believe any company could have really saved Palm, an acquisition by HTC or another up and comer in the space would have been interesting.

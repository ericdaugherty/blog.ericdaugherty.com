In a fast moving marketplace like Consumer&nbsp;Electronics, where your newest cellphone is out of date within a year and&nbsp;ancient&nbsp;after two, I thought it would be worthwhile celebrating some of the electronics that have served me well year after year.

Some of these are great investments because they are in a category that evolves more slowly. &nbsp;Others are examples where buying quality really pays off.

<h3>
Printers</h3>
First, while it is no longer with me, I want to celebrate again my HP 4L Laser Printer. &nbsp;I finally retired the printer in 2008 after 14 years of great service. &nbsp;I wrote about its <a href="http://blog.ericdaugherty.com/2008/04/hp-laserjet-4l-retired.html">retirement here</a>.

The HP 4L was replaced by a Dell 1320c, with a wired network connection. &nbsp;It has served well for 4 years now, and will hopefully continue for another 10. &nbsp;It was more expensive then an ink jet, but if it continues to serve it will be well worth it.

At its base, it provides access to a set of RAID drives over the network. &nbsp;But it also has a rich eco-system of software add-ons, and has been well maintained by ReadyNAS (now Netgear). &nbsp;With SSH access, it is really a small linux box, which in addition to providing file hosting also serves as my DynDNS client, linux shell access, SVN server, and&nbsp;occasional&nbsp;Bit Torrent client. &nbsp;At the time it replaced a full 'desktop' linux server, and I've never looked back. &nbsp;While I did have to replace the power supply last year, it has worked flawlessly otherwise, and I'm happy to upgrade a power supply every 4 years if it keeps working great.</div>
<h3>
Keys To Longevity</h3>
<div>
What are the keys to finding great electronic investments? &nbsp;First, the Printer and A/V Receiver categories are places where the core technology isn't&nbsp;evolving&nbsp;that quickly. &nbsp;In areas like these, I think it makes sense to spend the additional money up front for a quality, 'top of the line' product that will last. &nbsp;If you buy 'as much receiver as you can afford', it will likely last you a long time.</div>
<div>

</div>
<div>
Second, you need to be willing to augment them with accessories. &nbsp;While my receiver has several digital inputs, it didn't always have the right mix. &nbsp;I had to add a $30 Optical to Coax digital audio convert to connect up a new component. &nbsp;But a $30 accessory to keep a (original cost) $999 receiver working is a no-brainer.</div>
<div>

</div>
<div>
For my Dell 1320c printer, it has wired internet but no wireless. &nbsp;But again, with the purchase of a wireless adapter, I was able to extend the life of the printer.</div>
<div>

</div>
<div>
Third, flexibility extends a components life. &nbsp;By purchasing a receiver with a lot of inputs, it was able to continue to provide value over time. &nbsp;With the ReadyNAS NV+, I was able to evolve the features it provided, reducing the need to 'upgrade' to something newer.</div>
<div>

</div>
<div>
And finally, buy quality. &nbsp;At the time, there were less expensive options for each of these purchases. &nbsp;But buy spending a bit more for a&nbsp;workhorse&nbsp;device, I saved money in the long run.</div>

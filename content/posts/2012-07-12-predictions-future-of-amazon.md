As a customer and fan of Amazon, I've been watching the company grow and develop for a while. &nbsp;Based on my experiences and&nbsp;observations, I've come up with a picture of the not-so-distant future that I believe will (mostly) come to pass.<br />
<h2>


Today</h2>
<span style="background-color: white;">Setting aside their thriving Cloud Computing business, Amazon primarily sells consumer goods and digital entertainment (E-Books, Songs, Movies, TV Shows, and Android Applications).</span><br />
<br />
Amazon has created a strong incentive to purchase digital contents through their store. &nbsp;They offer hardware dedicated for e-books (the Kindle line) as well as a 7" Android tablet for apps, music, e-books, and video. &nbsp;However, they do not lock you into their platform. &nbsp;You can read an Amazon e-book on Android devices, iOS devices, and your computer. &nbsp;You can stream Amazon shows and movies to various set-top boxes. &nbsp;Compared to Apple or Google's online offering, they have the most complete and consumable content available, usually for the lowest price.<br />
<br />
For physical goods, they have created a similar draw. &nbsp;Their <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/prime">Amazon Prime</a> program is a genius approach to lock in customers. &nbsp;For $79 a year, your entire household can have everything they order from Amazon*&nbsp;delivered&nbsp;2nd day, FOR FREE. &nbsp;Couple this with the lack of Sales Tax, ordering something from Amazon is almost always cheaper, and usually more&nbsp;convenient&nbsp;than going to a retail store. &nbsp;If you are really in a hurry, you can pay $3.99 per item to have it delivered overnight.<br />
<br />
This eliminates most comparison shopping by Amazon Prime customers. &nbsp;If you've paid the $79, and are ordering it online, you will almost always start with Amazon, and usually will not spent time and effort&nbsp;comparison&nbsp;shopping.<br />
<br />
* As long as Amazon fulfills the order. &nbsp;When another store uses Amazon as a retail front but handles their own order processing, the Prime shipping is not available.<br />
<h2>


Tomorrow</h2>
<div>
<span style="background-color: white;">Amazon has been very careful about where it&nbsp;establishes&nbsp;a&nbsp;physical&nbsp;presence. &nbsp;It does this to avoid having to collect state and local sales taxes in most states. &nbsp;It has engaged in very public fights with states when they attempt to force Amazon to collect sales tax. &nbsp;This has primarily manifested itself in battles over its affiliate programs, with Amazon discontinuing the programs in any states that pass laws that cause affiliates to be considered a physical presence in the state.</span></div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
However, the writing is on the wall. &nbsp;Amazon will end up collecting sales taxes in most states. &nbsp;As this <a href="http://tech.fortune.cnn.com/2012/06/27/amazon-3/">Fortune article</a> states, Amazon already collects taxes in 5 states, and has agreed to add 8 more starting this year, through 2016. &nbsp;It is obvious at this point that they will eventually have to collect taxes in nearly every state.</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
The incentive to avoid a physical presences in states is&nbsp;disappearing, and I expect within 5 years will be gone completely. &nbsp;That opens up a host of new opportunities for Amazon.</div>
<h2>


The Future</h2>
<div>
In a future where physical location is irrelevant, Amazon is free to open up Stores, Warehouses, and Distribution Centers anywhere they like.<br />
<br />
But as I write this, I see I've already been preempted. &nbsp;<span style="background-color: white;">In fact, instead of describing what these will be, Slate has an article talking about same day delivery. &nbsp;</span><a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/business/small_business/2012/07/amazon_same_day_delivery_how_the_e_commerce_giant_will_destroy_local_retail_.html" style="background-color: white;">Go read the article at Slate</a>.&nbsp;(thanks&nbsp;<a href="http://daringfireball.net/linked/2012/07/12/amazon-manjoo" style="background-color: white;">DaringFireball</a><span style="background-color: white;">).</span><br />
<span style="background-color: white;"><br /></span><br />
<span style="background-color: white;">In short, Amazon is building a massive and advanced distribution center. &nbsp;They will put these in all major&nbsp;metropolitan&nbsp;areas, and will be able to fulfill orders same day.</span><br />
<span style="background-color: white;"><br /></span><br />
<span style="background-color: white;">I'm curious to see whether they maintain their shipper relationships (UPS), buy one, or build their own local courier/delivery service. &nbsp;But we will see a day within 5 years where you order an item on Amazon and it is delivered to your home the same day in a number of major cities.</span><br />
<span style="background-color: white;"><br /></span><br />
I also expect that Amazon will open up retail centers. &nbsp;As they drive all the big-box stores out of business, there will be plenty of retail locations for them to snap up cheaply. &nbsp;The centers will be show rooms for their massive online catalog, with&nbsp;convenient&nbsp;in person support and return. &nbsp;I envision a modern day <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_Merchandise">Service Merchandise</a> style store.</div>
<div>
<br />
For those folks concerned about big ticket purchases online, they can walk into the Amazon showroom, pick out a TV or other item, and have it delivered to their home that afternoon.<br />
<br />
If Amazon can pull this off, they will be THE major retailer, online or otherwise.</div>
